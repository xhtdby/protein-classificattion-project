# COMP0082 Coursework Report — Protein Enzyme Classification

## 1. Introduction

Enzymes are biological catalysts that underpin virtually every metabolic process in living
organisms. The Enzyme Commission (EC) numbering system classifies enzymes into six top-level
classes—Oxidoreductases (EC 1), Transferases (EC 2), Hydrolases (EC 3), Lyases (EC 4),
Isomerases (EC 5), and Ligases (EC 6)—according to the reaction they catalyse. Computational
prediction of enzyme class from amino acid sequence alone is of substantial practical
importance: it accelerates the functional annotation of the millions of uncharacterised
proteins generated by high-throughput sequencing, and it supports drug target identification,
metabolic engineering, and synthetic biology. As genome sequencing throughput continues to
outpace experimental characterisation, automated enzyme classification becomes increasingly
essential for bridging the sequence–function gap.

This project addresses the problem as a seven-class supervised classification task, where
class 0 represents "not an enzyme" and classes 1–6 correspond to the six EC classes. The
dataset comprises 39 764 non-homologous protein sequences with a heavily skewed distribution:
class 0 constitutes 81.5 % of the data, while the smallest class (Ligase, EC 6) accounts
for only 0.7 %. This extreme class imbalance—spanning two orders of magnitude between the
largest and smallest classes—is the central challenge of the project and informs every design
decision, from loss weighting and oversampling strategies to the choice of evaluation metrics.

The approach progresses systematically from handcrafted sequence-based features through protein
language model (PLM) embeddings to an ensemble that combines the complementary strengths of
gradient-boosted trees and end-to-end fine-tuned transformers. All experiments use stratified
five-fold cross-validation with strict data-leakage prevention, and all results are reported
with standard deviations across folds to quantify variability.

## 2. Methods

### 2.1 Feature Engineering

Four groups of features were implemented, ordered by increasing representational power.

**Amino acid composition (20-d).** The normalised frequency of each canonical amino acid,
capturing the global residue distribution. This is the simplest possible sequence
representation and serves as a well-established baseline in protein classification literature.

**Dipeptide frequencies (400-d).** Normalised counts of all ordered amino acid pairs,
encoding local sequential context that single-residue composition cannot capture. Combined
with a single sequence-length scalar, this yields a 421-dimensional handcrafted composition
vector.

**Physicochemical properties (8-d).** Molecular weight, isoelectric point, aromaticity
(Phe + Trp + Tyr fraction), GRAVY hydropathicity, charge at pH 7.0, and Chou–Fasman
secondary-structure propensity fractions (helix, turn, sheet). These were computed with
BioPython's `ProtParam` module. Unlike composition features, these capture higher-level
biophysical properties that may distinguish enzyme families with different catalytic
environments.

**Protein language model embeddings.** ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2023), a transformer-based PLM
pre-trained on millions of protein sequences via masked language modelling, was used as a
feature extractor. Two model sizes were evaluated: the 8-million-parameter variant
(`esm2_t6_8M_UR50D`, 320-d output) and the 650-million-parameter variant
(`esm2_t33_650M_UR50D`, 1 280-d output). For each sequence, the final-layer hidden states
were mean-pooled across all non-special tokens to produce a single dense vector. Because the
ESM-2 forward pass is entirely stateless—no label information is used—embeddings were safely
pre-computed once and cached to disk. The key advantage of PLM embeddings over handcrafted
features is that they encode evolutionary and structural information learned from millions of
diverse protein families, capturing patterns that are impossible to design manually.

### 2.2 Experimental Design

**Cross-validation protocol.** All experiments used stratified five-fold cross-validation
(seed = 42), guaranteeing that every fold preserved the original class proportions.

**Data-leakage prevention.** Three strict rules were enforced: (i) `StandardScaler` was fit
exclusively on the training fold and applied to both folds via `.transform()`; (ii) SMOTE
oversampling was applied only to the training fold; (iii) features were derived solely from
the amino acid sequence, never from the label.

**Metrics.** Four complementary metrics were recorded for every experiment: accuracy, macro F1,
balanced accuracy, and the Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC). Macro F1 was used as the
primary model-selection criterion because it penalises poor performance on minority classes
without being dominated by the majority class.

### 2.3 Models

**Baseline models.** Logistic Regression (`class_weight='balanced'`, multinomial, L-BFGS
solver, `max_iter=1000`) and Random Forest (`n_estimators=300`, `class_weight='balanced'`)
were trained on the 429-dimensional handcrafted feature vector. A pipeline variant applied
PCA to 50 components before the Random Forest to mitigate the curse of dimensionality imposed
by the 400-d dipeptide features.

**Gradient-boosted trees.** XGBoost and LightGBM were trained on ESM-2 650M embeddings
concatenated with the 8-d physicochemical features (1 288-d total). Hyperparameters for
XGBoost were: `max_depth=6`, `n_estimators=800`, `learning_rate=0.1`, `subsample=0.8`,
`colsample_bytree=0.9`. Class imbalance was addressed through balanced sample weights and,
separately, through SMOTE on the training fold (five nearest neighbours, default ratio).

**End-to-end fine-tuning.** The ESM-2 8M model was fine-tuned with all transformer layers
unfrozen. A classification head—`Linear(320, 128) → GELU → Dropout(0.3) → Linear(128, 7)`—
was appended after mean-pooling. Training used AdamW with differential learning rates
(backbone: 2 × 10⁻⁵; head: 1 × 10⁻⁴), OneCycleLR scheduling, bfloat16 mixed precision,
gradient accumulation to an effective batch size of 16, and early stopping on validation
macro F1 with patience of 5 epochs. Per-fold class weights were computed from training-fold
frequencies and applied to the cross-entropy loss.

**Ensemble.** A soft-vote ensemble combined the out-of-fold (OOF) probability vectors of the
fine-tuned ESM-2 8M model and the XGBoost + SMOTE model. Weights were set proportional to
each model's individual OOF macro F1 (w_finetune ≈ 0.46, w_xgboost ≈ 0.54). Per-class
decision thresholds were optimised on the blended OOF probabilities via
`scipy.optimize.minimize`.

### 2.4 Feature Ablation

To quantify the contribution of each feature group, XGBoost (balanced sample weights, no SMOTE)
was trained on four feature combinations: (A) handcrafted only (429-d), (B) ESM-2 650M only
(1 280-d), (C) ESM-2 + all handcrafted (1 709-d), and (D) ESM-2 + physicochemical only
(1 288-d).

## 4. Interpretability

Four interpretability techniques were applied to the best single model (XGBoost + SMOTE).

**Feature importance.** Gini-based importance ranking shows that ESM-2 embedding dimensions
dominate the top 20 features. The highest-ranked handcrafted feature is molecular weight
(rank 5), confirming that ESM-2 subsumes most biochemical information.

**SHAP analysis.** TreeExplainer SHAP values (computed on a 500-sequence subsample) corroborate
the Gini ranking: ESM-2 dimensions 112, 308, and 216 have the highest mean absolute SHAP
values across all seven classes.

**Per-class precision, recall, and F1** (see Table 4 below) reveal that the model is

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 What Worked

The single most impactful decision was using ESM-2 650M embeddings as features for
gradient-boosted trees, which lifted macro F1 from 0.19 (handcrafted baseline) to 0.58—a
threefold improvement. This confirms that pre-trained protein language models encode rich
functional information that handcrafted sequence statistics fail to capture. The ESM-2 model,
trained on evolutionary protein data, has implicitly learned to represent structural folds,
binding site motifs, and conserved catalytic residues—all of which are highly relevant to
enzyme classification but impossible to engineer from sequence alone.

SMOTE on the training fold provided a consistent +2 percentage-point improvement in macro F1
without harming accuracy, demonstrating that synthetic minority oversampling is beneficial
even for PLM-based features. This result is noteworthy because SMOTE operates in the
1 288-dimensional embedding space, where the interpolation between minority-class samples
generates plausible synthetic enzyme representations rather than arbitrary noise.

The soft-vote ensemble achieved the highest macro F1 (0.625) by combining two models that
process sequences through fundamentally different pathways: XGBoost on frozen 650M embeddings
captures static feature interactions via decision-tree splits, while the fine-tuned 8M model
learns task-specific representations through gradient-based backpropagation. Their OOF
prediction errors are partially uncorrelated, enabling genuine complementarity. Notably, the
ensemble's balanced accuracy (0.604) substantially exceeds that of either constituent model,
suggesting that the two models make different mistakes on minority-class samples.

### 5.2 Limitations

**Minority-class performance.** Lyase (F1 = 0.24) and Isomerase (F1 = 0.29) remain
challenging despite all imbalance-handling strategies. With only 600 and 411 training examples
respectively, these classes are fundamentally under-represented. The model's precision for
Isomerase (0.554) suggests it can reliably identify some members of this class, but its low
recall (0.200) indicates it fails to recognise the majority of Isomerases. More aggressive
strategies—focal loss to down-weight easy majority-class examples, class-conditional data
augmentation, or hierarchical classification (first enzyme vs. non-enzyme, then among EC
classes)—may yield improvements.

**Fine-tuning gap.** The fine-tuned ESM-2 8M model (macro F1 = 0.51) underperforms XGBoost
on frozen ESM-2 650M embeddings (0.60). This is attributable to two factors: (i) the 8M
model has far less representational capacity than the 650M model (6 vs. 33 transformer
layers), and (ii) end-to-end fine-tuning on a relatively small dataset (39 764 sequences)
with 8 million trainable parameters risks overfitting despite regularisation (dropout,
weight decay, early stopping). Fine-tuning the 650M model would likely close this gap but
requires substantially more GPU memory (24 GB+).

**Handcrafted feature noise.** Adding the full 429-d handcrafted vector to ESM-2 embeddings
slightly degraded performance (macro F1: 0.552 vs. 0.562 for ESM-2 alone). The 400-d
dipeptide features, while informative in isolation, become redundant in the presence of PLM
embeddings and introduce noise dimensions that XGBoost partially overfits to. The 8-d
physicochemical subset, by contrast, provides complementary signal (macro F1: 0.575),
confirming that carefully selected biophysical descriptors retain value even alongside
powerful PLM representations.

### 5.3 Error Analysis

The confusion matrix reveals biologically interpretable patterns. Hydrolase is sometimes
confused with Transferase; this is plausible because both catalyse bond cleavage, differing
primarily in whether the cleaved group is transferred to water (hydrolysis) or to another
acceptor molecule (transfer). Lyase and Isomerase errors predominantly default to Not enzyme,
reflecting the model's conservative behaviour under class imbalance—when uncertain, it
predicts the overwhelming majority class.

Of 5 312 total misclassifications, 1 499 (28.2 %) occur at high confidence (p ≥ 0.80).
These high-confidence errors are concentrated on class 0 predictions (785 errors), suggesting
that the model occasionally assigns very high probability to the majority class even for
genuine enzymes. This is consistent with the well-documented calibration challenges of
tree-based models on imbalanced data, where the learned decision boundaries are biased toward
the majority class.

## 6. Conclusion

This project demonstrates that protein language model embeddings, combined with
gradient-boosted trees and careful class-imbalance handling, achieve strong performance
on seven-class enzyme classification from sequence alone. The systematic progression from a
macro F1 of 0.19 (handcrafted baseline) to 0.625 (ensemble) confirms the dominance of
learned protein representations over traditional feature engineering. The ensemble of a
fine-tuned ESM-2 8M model and XGBoost on frozen 650M embeddings exploits complementary model
strengths and achieves the best overall performance across all four evaluation metrics.

The ablation study demonstrates that ESM-2 embeddings are the dominant source of predictive
power, while a small set of physicochemical features provides complementary biophysical
signal. SMOTE oversampling on training folds consistently improves minority-class recall
without degrading overall accuracy. Confidence calibration confirms that the model is
well-calibrated, with high-confidence predictions achieving 95 % accuracy.

Key directions for future work include: (i) fine-tuning the larger 650M model, which would
likely close the fine-tuning gap and further improve ensemble performance; (ii) applying focal
loss or cost-sensitive learning to improve performance on the most difficult minority classes
(Lyase, Isomerase); and (iii) hierarchical classification—first distinguishing enzyme from
non-enzyme, then classifying among the six EC classes—which could exploit the natural two-level
structure of the label space.

The complete codebase, including the blind prediction pipeline, is fully reproducible with a
single seed (42) and ready for deployment on unseen test data.
